    What you're looking at

    Each solution path trivializes a stabilized balanced group presentation using two ingredients specific to this project — a change of variables and a substitution supermove — rather than the classical Andrews–Curtis (Miller–Schupp) moves. This explorer replays those paths step by step.

    Change of variables z = w

    We start from a 2-generator presentation ⟨x, y | r₁, r₂⟩ and add a third generator z defined as a word w(x, y):

    ⟨x, y | r₁, r₂⟩  ⟶  ⟨x, y, z | r₁, r₂, z·w⁻¹⟩

    The defining word w can be anything — r₁, r₂, x, y, or an arbitrary word like xy. The same presentation looks and solves differently under different w, which is why the player has a z = w selector — switch it to compare. The explorer decodes the actual w straight from the initial state, so it always shows the real change of variables, whatever word you ran.

    Substitution supermove

    A single step replaces one relator by the freely- and cyclically-reduced splice of a cyclic rotation of one relator with a cyclic rotation of another relator (or its inverse). It is a composite of classical AC moves, taken as one atomic step. In the player, each step highlights the one relator that changed — old → new — since exactly one relator is substituted per step.

    The player also replays each move in slow motion, phase by phase (Roles → Invert → Rotate → Splice → Cancel → Settle, tracked by the chips above the presentation). Relators are cyclic words, so each operand row carries a small ring drawing the relator as the circle it really is: rotating a relator never changes the word — it only moves the cut marker that decides where the ring is cut open to write the row, and the tiles slide with wrap-around to match. If the move uses the partner's inverse, its tiles mirror and case-flip in place. The two rotated words then glue — partner tiles fly across into the changed row, with a seam marking where the halves met — and inverse pairs cancel one at a time, zipping shut at the seam (a dashed arc appears when the cancellation wraps around the cyclic word's two ends). This factorization is reconstructed from the stored states and move tuple by re-running the actual move rule, and is only shown when it reproduces the stored next state exactly (verified for 100% of the bundled paths). Use the Speed control (or Instant to skip) and ↻ Replay move to watch any step again.

    Each relator keeps a fixed row for the whole path, so only the substituted relator visibly changes on a step (the stored states are canonical and reordered, but the player threads each relator's identity onto a stable row). The raw move tuple (slot, ra_rot, c_rot, c_inv) alone does not pin down the operand pair; the shown factorization is one that provably reproduces the next state.

    Where each presentation comes from

    MS(1190) splits into two subsets, badged on every card: Original 640 (idx 0–639) — searched directly under the 2-generator baseline and all four z = w words at 500,000 nodes — and Hard 550 (idx 640–1189). The hard presentations were not searched one by one: up to AC-equivalence they collapse into 261 unsolved classes, and the representative of each class was searched under z ∈ {r₁, r₂, x, y} at 500,000 nodes (the Comparison tab computes the outcome from the loaded data). 544 of the 550 carry a Searched via rep pill linking them to their class — click one to open its representative. The remaining 6 have no class link in the paper's table and stay Not attempted. Every section counts all 1190 presentations.

    The (n, w) grid lays the whole Miller–Schupp family out as a spreadsheet (rows = the word w, columns = n): green cells trivialized in the original run, and each remaining cell reduces to one of the 261 unsolved-class representatives (the ms_reps_unsolved dataset) — click any labelled cell to open it and compare each change of variables' search cost.

    Goal: the trivial presentation

    With three generators, "solved" means reducing to three length-one relators — ⟨x, y, z | x, y, z⟩ (total length 3), the trivial group. This is different from the 2-generator length-2 goal.

    The Baseline and Comparison tabs

    Baseline shows GS-Sub: the same greedy best-first search over substitution supermoves, run on the plain 2-generator presentation with no z = w stabilization. It is the control every z = w arm is measured against. Comparison puts them head-to-head on the 640 solved MS(1190): coverage, nodes explored, and path length — per arm, and per presentation on the presentations both solve (points below the y = x line mean the change of variables did better). Comparison's verdict panel states the current answer in plain English, with every number computed from the loaded data. Note one structural limit: the 2-generator baseline was never run on the hard set, so "does stabilization crack presentations the baseline cannot solve?" cannot be answered from this bundle — answering it on the hard classes is the open research question.
