Answers four questions:

2. Is it related to the **126 classes**?
3. **Does the 126-class finding prove the 261 are AC-trivial?** ← the one that matters
4. Is the **main table in `EQUIVALENCE_FINDING.md` false**, given that `pres_id` 202 and 260 reach

## 0. The short answers

**Q3 — does 126 prove the 261 are AC-trivial?**

Your question contains a false premise, and the false premise *is* the confusion:

> "does showing that 126 of them are AC trivial prove that all 261 are AC trivial"

**Nothing among the 261 has been shown AC-trivial. Not one. The count of trivializations is zero.**

The 126 is **not** a solved subset of the 261. It is a **partition** of all 261 into 126 groups.
Nothing was solved; the presentations were *sorted*. So the literal question has no answer, because
the thing it supposes never happened.

The conditional version is the real one, and its answer is **yes**:

> **If** you proved one representative of each of the 126 classes AC-trivial, **then** all 261
> would follow — by certificates you can check by hand.

The classes tell you **which searches are redundant**. They never tell you **how a search comes out**.

**Q4 — is the main table false?** **No — the table is correct, and I verified it.** But you have
found something real: it refutes a *different* claim in the document, and that claim is one
`EQUIVALENCE_FINDING.md` itself calls "the strongest AC statement in this document." See **§6**.

## 2. What the 126 classes actually are

### The relation is ACA, not AC

Three relations. Blurring them is the easiest mistake here:

| relation | what it rewrites | is it what the 126 counts? |
|---|---|---|
| **AC move** (AC1–AC3) | the **relators** — `rᵢ → rᵢrⱼ`, `rᵢ → g rᵢ g⁻¹`, `rᵢ → rᵢ⁻¹` | not on its own |
| **change of variables** (`φ ∈ Aut(F₂)`, e.g. `x ↦ xy`) | the **generators**, applied to both relators at once. **Not an AC move.** | not on its own |
| **ACA** | generated by **both** | ✅ **this one** |

### Why ACA is the right currency

The load-bearing fact (`EQUIVALENCE_FINDING.md` §0):

> **`P` is AC-trivial ⟺ `φ(P)` is AC-trivial.**

Because AC moves commute with `φ` (it's a homomorphism: `φ(rᵢrⱼ) = φ(rᵢ)φ(rⱼ)`), an AC path
`P → (x, y)` maps to an AC path `φ(P) → (φ(x), φ(y))` — and `(φ(x), φ(y))` is a **basis** of F₂,
hence Nielsen- (= AC-) equivalent to `(x, y)`. The AC-class *of the trivial presentation* is
φ-invariant.

So two ACA-equivalent presentations are **the same problem**: solve one, you have settled the other.
That is exactly the property that makes running both a waste.

### What it does not mean

**ACA-equivalent is not AC-equivalent.** An ACA merge does *not* exhibit a path of AC moves joining
the two. The chain you'd need — `P ~AC trivial ~AC φ(trivial) ~AC φ(P)` — has a first link that
**requires `P` to already be AC-trivial**, which is precisely the open question. If `P` is a genuine
counterexample, the chain never starts.

(There is a real exception to this in the data — two pairs *are* joined by a raw AC path. That is
§6, and the document currently denies it.)

## 3. So what are the classes *for*? — the soundness/incompleteness asymmetry

This is the part that makes the number useful.

The search is **sound but incomplete**:

- **Sound** — every merge ships a machine-checked certificate. `verify_proofs.py` re-derives all 135
  edges from `certificates.json` + the raw CSV alone, sharing no code with the search, and rebuilds
  the partition from the verified edges. A merge that does not verify is not reported.
- **Incomplete** — the search may have **missed** merges. A budget or a length cap removes edges,
  never adds them. So 126 is an **upper bound**: the true number of distinct problems is **≤ 126**.

Read those together — the asymmetry is the entire payoff:

| direction | consequence |
|---|---|
| **Soundness** — every reported merge is real | Running **one representative per class covers all 261.** Airtight. |
| **Incompleteness** — merges may be missing | There may be **fewer than 126** real problems. Cost: some **redundant** work. It can **never** make you miss a presentation. |

> **The error runs only in the safe direction.** Being wrong about 126 means you did too *much*
> work, never too little. That is why 126 is a safe target count.

### The four things the classes buy

1. **Compute.** 261 runs answered at most 126 questions — **52% of that 1M-node sweep was duplicated
   compute.** The largest class has **8** members: eight separate million-node runs of one problem
   (`{18_6, 18_7, 21_1, 21_36, 22_13, 22_14, 23_21, 23_24}`). 113 of the 126 classes have more than
   one member; only 13 presentations are alone. Total relator length to run: **5,204 → 2,488**.
2. **Better-conditioned coordinates.** The `Aut`-minimal representative is the shortest member, and
   greedy is length-guided. (`EQUIVALENCE_FINDING.md` argues this; the plan flags it as **untested —
   nobody has run them**.)
3. **The honest denominator.** Any future claim "we cracked *k* of the unsolved set" must be counted
   in **classes**, not rows — else cracking one problem that has 8 coordinate systems reads as 8
   results.
4. **A correct sampling pool.** `STABLE_AC_PIPELINE_PLAN.md` already relies on this: *"The pool is
   the 126 ACA classes — not the 261 rows."*

### And the one thing they do not buy

**No progress whatsoever toward proving any of them trivial.** There is **no known invariant that
separates the remaining classes** — all present the trivial group, all have `|det| = 1`, so both
available invariants are blind. 126 bounds how many *questions* there are. It says nothing about the
*answers*.

## 4. Lemma 11, line by line

Source: **arXiv:2408.15332 §9.1** — `literature/txt/math_ml_paper_2408.15332.txt:2499`.

### The moves it is written in

Standard AC moves on a balanced presentation (paper, ~line 296):

- **(AC1)** substitute some `rᵢ` by `rᵢrⱼ`, `i ≠ j`
- **(AC2)** replace some `rᵢ` by `rᵢ⁻¹`
- **(AC3)** change some `rᵢ` to `g rᵢ g⁻¹`, `g` a generator or its inverse

**Stable** AC adds two (paper, §9.1):

- **(AC4)** *stabilization* — add a new generator **and a trivial relator**:
  `⟨x₁…xₙ | r₁…rₙ⟩ → ⟨x₁…xₙ, xₙ₊₁ | r₁…rₙ, xₙ₊₁⟩`
- **(AC5)** *destabilization* — the inverse of AC4

Presentations related by AC1–AC5 are **stably AC-equivalent**. Note AC4 permits only the relator
`xₙ₊₁` — **a single letter**. Remember that; it is exactly what Lemma 11 relaxes.

**Definition 1 (Substitution)** — paper, line 357 — is the other prerequisite. If a relator is
literally `w⁻¹w′` (i.e. "`w = w′`"), a sequence of AC-moves can replace any occurrence of `w` in
*another* relator by `w′`, at a cost **linear** in the word lengths. This one is effective — you can
actually run it.

### The statement

> **Lemma 11 (Substitution and Removal).** Let `P = ⟨x₁,…,xₙ, y | r₁,…,rₙ, y⁻¹w⟩` be a presentation
> of the trivial group, where `w` is a word in `x₁,…,xₙ`. Then `P′ = ⟨x₁,…,xₙ | r′₁,…,r′ₙ⟩` is
> **stably AC-equivalent** to `P`, where `r′ᵢ` is `rᵢ` with all occurrences of `y` replaced by `w`.

In plain terms: `P` has one extra generator `y`, and one of its relators, `y⁻¹w`, is nothing but the
**definition** `y = w`. Lemma 11 says you may **eliminate `y`** — substitute `w` for `y` everywhere,
delete the generator, delete its defining relator — and land on something stably AC-equivalent.

That the *group* is unchanged is elementary (a Tietze transformation). The content of Lemma 11 is
stronger: the elimination is realizable **by AC moves**, so it is legal inside the AC world, where
you may not use arbitrary isomorphisms.

### The proof, step by step

**Step 1 — substitute (an honest, effective AC move).**
The relator `y⁻¹w` *is* Definition 1's `w′⁻¹w` with `w′ = y`. So AC moves let you replace every `y`
in `r₁,…,rₙ` by `w`:

```
P̃ = ⟨x₁,…,xₙ, y | r′₁,…,r′ₙ, y⁻¹w⟩
```

`P̃ ~AC P`, with a linear move count. Nothing hidden yet.

**Step 2 — `P′` presents the trivial group.**
The `r′ᵢ` now contain **no `y`**. So

```
ϕ : P̃ ⟶ P′,     ϕ(xᵢ) = xᵢ,     ϕ(y) = w
```

is well-defined — each relator of `P̃` maps to `1`: `ϕ(r′ᵢ) = r′ᵢ = 1` in `P′`, and
`ϕ(y⁻¹w) = w⁻¹w = 1` — and **surjective**, since it hits every `xᵢ`. `P̃` is trivial (P was, and AC
moves don't change the group). A surjective image of the trivial group is trivial. **So `P′` is
trivial.**

**Step 3 — therefore `w` is a product of conjugates of the `r′ᵢ`.**
`P′` trivial means the normal closure of `r′₁,…,r′ₙ` is **all** of `F(x₁,…,xₙ)`. And `w` lives in
that free group. So `w` lies in the normal closure:

```
w = w₁ (r′ᵢ₁)^{±1} w₁⁻¹ · w₂ (r′ᵢ₂)^{±1} w₂⁻¹ ⋯ w_m (r′ᵢ_m)^{±1} w_m⁻¹
```

**The paper flags at once that `m` may be much larger than `n`.** Hold that — it is the whole catch.

**Step 4 — turn the relator `y⁻¹w` into the single letter `y`, then delete it.**
Write `Π` for that product, so `w = Π` in the free group. Inside `P̃`:

- each `r′ᵢ` **is a relator of `P̃`**, so "multiply the relator by a conjugate of `r′ᵢ^{±1}`" is a
  legal (AC1 + AC3) combination;
- AC2 inverts `y⁻¹w` into `w⁻¹y`;
- apply the multiplication `m` times, building `Π` on the left:

```
w₁(r′ᵢ₁)^{±1}w₁⁻¹ ⋯ w_m(r′ᵢ_m)^{±1}w_m⁻¹ · w⁻¹ · y     =     Π · w⁻¹ · y
```

- and since `Π = w`, this **freely reduces to** `w · w⁻¹ · y = y`.

The relator is now literally the single letter `y` — precisely the shape **AC5** removes. Delete it
and the generator, and you are left with `⟨x₁,…,xₙ | r′₁,…,r′ₙ⟩ = P′`. ∎

### What it needs, and what it refuses to give

**It needs `P` to present the trivial group.** The hypothesis is used twice — Step 2 and Step 3. It
is not a general fact about presentations. The Miller–Schupp family satisfies it (they *are* balanced
presentations of the trivial group), so it applies here.

**It is non-effective — and the paper says so itself:**

> For substitution, the number of AC moves constituting the supermove is a linear function of the
> lengths of the relators. In contrast, **our proof of Lemma 11 does not yield a similar result** for
> the substitution and removal move **due to the absence of a bound on `m`**. Finding this bound …
> **would be very useful.**

Step 3 proves the decomposition **exists** by a normal-closure argument. It does not construct the
`wₖ`, and bounds nothing. The repo already records this — `experiments/greedy_tests/test_stable_ac.py:176`:
*"Lemma 11's removal is proved with an unbounded number of substitutions."*

**Consequence:** Lemma 11 tells you an AC path exists. It does **not** hand you one, and it cannot be
executed as a search move with a cost model. It is a **licence, not an algorithm.** The mentor's
4-pager calls it *"a real supermove"* and, in the same breath, *"nontrivial to realize in terms of the
stable AC moves in general."*

**And it proves nothing trivial.** Lemma 11 relates `P` to `P′`. It does not claim either can be
trivialized. The paper is explicit that the Akbulut–Kirby and Miller–Schupp families *"also serve as
potential counterexamples to the **stable** Andrews–Curtis conjecture."* Stable AC is weaker — and
**also open**.

## 5. Is Lemma 11 related to the 126 classes?

**Not to how they were produced. It sits one layer above them, and it is the tool for the *next*
pipeline.** `EQUIVALENCE_FINDING.md` says so:

> **Stabilization (AC4/AC5, adding a generator `z`) is not used anywhere here.** Stable-AC is coarser
> still and could merge more; that is what `STABLE_AC_PIPELINE_PLAN.md` is for.

| layer | moves | generators | classes over the 261 | status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| change of variables only | `Aut(F₂)` | 2 | **168** | **exact** — a wall (Whitehead decides it) |
| **ACA** = AC + change of variables | AC1–AC3 + `Aut(F₂)` | 2 | **126** | **upper bound** — ← the finding you asked about |
| **stable AC** — Lemma 11's home | AC1–**AC5** | 2 **→ 3** | not yet computed | the next pipeline |

### Where it does connect: Branch A

Instantiate Lemma 11 at `n = 2`, calling the extra generator `z`:

```
P  = ⟨x, y, z | r₁, r₂, z⁻¹w⟩          w any word in x, y
```

`r₁, r₂` contain **no `z`**, so the substitution `z ↦ w` does nothing to them: `r′ᵢ = rᵢ`. Hence

```
P′ = ⟨x, y | r₁, r₂⟩                   — the original presentation, untouched
```

and Lemma 11 gives, for **any** word `w`:

> **`⟨x, y, z | r₁, r₂, z⁻¹w⟩` is stably AC-equivalent to `⟨x, y | r₁, r₂⟩`.**

That single line *is* Branch A. `STABLE_AC_PIPELINE_PLAN.md` names it exactly that — *"the dumbest
stable-AC baseline (mentor idea #2; **Lemma-11 form**)"*. The payoff: **if the 3-generator search
trivializes `⟨x,y,z | r₁, r₂, z⁻¹w⟩`, the original 2-generator presentation is stably AC-trivial** —
a real result, and for AK(3) specifically an **open** one.

**Why this needs Lemma 11 and not just AC4.** Plain AC4 only lets you add the relator `z` — a single
letter. Lemma 11 licenses `z⁻¹w` for an **arbitrary** `w`, and that is where all the new search
freedom lives. It is also exactly the freedom whose AC-realization is non-effective (unbounded `m`).
You get the freedom; you do not get a bounded path.

### Could Lemma 11 shrink the 126 further?

**In the project's framing, yes — it is follow-up item #1** (`EQUIVALENCE_FINDING.md`): *"Strictly
coarser than ACA, so it can only merge more — and it is the one relation in this hierarchy that has
not been tried."* Two honest caveats:

- You **cannot** run Lemma 11 as a merging move — `m` is unbounded. You would instead search at
  `n_gen = 3` and see what meets what.
- Merging classes further is a *bonus*. The prize is **trivializing** one.

### The trap: two different things are called "change of variables"

| | **ACA's** change of variables | **Lemma 11 / Branch B's** |
|---|---|---|
| what | `φ ∈ Aut(F₂)`, an automorphism of the **free group** | stabilize → substitute → isolate → destabilize |
| invertible? | yes, by definition | not an `Aut(F₂)` map at all |
| uses the relators? | **no** — pure relabelling, blind to `r₁, r₂` | **yes** — uses a relator to *solve for* a generator |
| generators | stays 2 | 2 → 3 → back to 2, on a **new** pair `⟨y, z⟩` |
| effective? | yes — Whitehead decides it exactly | needs a lucky `w`; the removal is non-effective |
| where | produced the **168**, and the 93 `cv` merges | `STABLE_AC_PIPELINE_PLAN.md` Branch B |

The 4-pager's AK(3) example makes Branch B concrete: introduce `z = xyx`, substitute into `r₁` to get
`z = yxy`, **use that relator to isolate `x = y⁻¹zy⁻¹`**, then apply Lemma 11 to remove `x`. You end
at `⟨y, z | …⟩`. An `Aut(F₂)` map could never do that — it never looks at the relators.

## 6. Your 202 / 260 observation — what it does and does not break

**Your data is right, and I reproduced it.** `pres_id` **202** and **260** in
`greedy_1000000_261_mrl48_cyc_all_07_09_26.jsonl` record the **identical** `min_relator`:

```
202 = 18_9    min_relator = ['YYYxxyX', 'YXYXyXyxxYx']   len 18
260 = 19_52   min_relator = ['YYYxxyX', 'YXYXyXyxxYx']   len 18
```

And the "one other" you half-remembered is real — there are **exactly two** such collisions in the
261, no more:

```
210 = 18_11   min_relator = ['YYYxyXX', 'YXYXXyxyxYx']   len 18
235 = 19_46   min_relator = ['YYYxyXX', 'YXYXXyxyxYx']   len 18
```

(`max_relator` collisions: **0**. So this is not a systematic duplication artifact.)

**These are genuine AC-equivalences, in the strongest sense available.** Both roots reach the *same
state* by S-moves, and `EQUIVALENCE_FINDING.md`'s own soundness paragraph licenses the inference:
*"AC-equivalence is an equivalence relation, so `P →* Z` and `Q →* Z` gives `P ~ Q`."* So
**`18_9 ~AC 19_52`** and **`18_11 ~AC 19_46`** — **with no change of variables anywhere.**

### The main table is **not** false — I checked it

| table row | claim | re-verified |
|---|---|---|
| exact `(r1,r2)` | 261 distinct, 0 collisions | ✅ **261 / 261, 0 collisions** |
| canonical root (`canon_pair`) | 261 distinct, 0 collisions | ✅ **261 / 261, 0 collisions** |
| + `Aut(F₂)` | 168 | (unchanged) |
| + AC modulo `Aut(F₂)` | **126** | (unchanged — all four sit in **class 4**) |

The reason there is no contradiction: **rows 1–2 count distinct *roots*, not AC-classes.** Two
presentations can be AC-equivalent and still be entirely different words — being AC-equivalent means
their AC-*balls* eventually intersect, not that they start at the same place. AC-equivalence only
enters the ladder at the last row, and there the merge **is** recorded: `18_9`, `18_11`, `19_46`,
`19_52` are all in one 4-member class:

```
class 4:  n=4  members = [18_11, 18_9, 19_46, 19_52]
```

**The 126 count is unaffected.** The doc even credits these two merges explicitly — §4 lists *"The 2
AC merges the 1M-node sweep found by accident (`19_52≡18_9`, `19_46≡18_11`) — **2/2** rediscovered"* —
and uses them as an external check on the search.

### But you *have* found a real error — a different one

`EQUIVALENCE_FINDING.md` §5 says, of its 6 pure-AC certificate edges:

> `ψ` is never the identity, so **not one of the 261 pairs is joined by a raw AC path** — the AC path
> always lands on a relabelled partner.

**That sentence is false, and it contradicts §3 and §4 of the same document.**

- Your two collisions **are** raw AC paths — no `ψ`, no relabelling, no change of variables.
- §3 already says so: *"the AC path between them **climbs above total length 30**"* — asserting the
  raw AC path between `19_52` and `18_9` **exists** (it is just too high a hump for a length-capped
  BFS to cross, which is the measurement §3 exists to make).
- §4 already calls them *"the 2 **AC** merges."*

**Why §5 got it wrong:** it reasoned only from the ACA search's own certificate set. That search
expands one `Aut`-minimal representative per class, so its paths *naturally* land on relabelled
partners — `ψ ≠ identity` is a property **of the search**, not of the 261. §5 then generalized from
the search's output to a claim about the presentations, and forgot the two raw merges §3/§4 credit.

**A second, smaller consequence:** the shipped spanning tree for class 4 uses the edges
`18_9—19_46` (`aca`), `19_46—19_52` (`aca`), `18_9—18_11` (`aut`) — **none of which is one of the two
raw-AC pairs.** The certificate set is therefore *weaker than the data supports*: two raw AC
equivalences were available and the proof book records relabelled ones instead. The partition is
unchanged; the strength of the claim is not.

I have corrected §5 in `EQUIVALENCE_FINDING.md`. **This does not move the 126.**

## 7. What would actually settle the 261

Exhibit a trivialization — an explicit AC path to `⟨x, y | x, y⟩` — for one representative of each of
the **126** classes. The certificates then propagate it to all 261.

Today: **0 of 126 done**, at a million nodes per presentation.

What the equivalence work changed is only the **shape of the job**:

- it is **126 problems, not 261** — proved, not estimated;
- run the `Aut`-minimal representative, not the shipped coordinates;
- report in **classes**, not rows;
- and if a counterexample is ever confirmed, it is a counterexample to **one of 126** questions.

> **A partition tells you how many questions there are. It never tells you an answer.**
