## 2. Nielsen moves and witnesses

Let \((u_0,u_1)\) be an ordered pair of freely reduced words in
\(F(x,y)\). For \(i\ne j\), define four multiplication moves targeting
component \(i\):

\[
 \begin{array}{rcl}
 u_i&\mapsto&\overline{u_i u_j},\\
 u_i&\mapsto&\overline{u_i u_j^{-1}},\\
 u_i&\mapsto&\overline{u_j u_i},\\
 u_i&\mapsto&\overline{u_j^{-1}u_i}.
 \end{array}
\]

Together with swapping components and inverting either component, these are
the elementary Nielsen transformations. Every one is an automorphism of the
ordered generating tuple, and every one has an elementary inverse.

Call a pair **signed standard** if its components are distinct signed
generators:

\[
 (u_0,u_1)\in
 \{x,x^{-1},y,y^{-1}\}^2,
 \qquad |u_0|=|u_1|=1,
 \qquad \operatorname{und}(u_0)\ne \operatorname{und}(u_1)
\]

where \(\operatorname{und}\) forgets the sign.

### Theorem 2.1 (strict rank-two Nielsen reduction) — PROVEN

An ordered pair \((u,v)\) is a free basis of \(F(x,y)\) if and only if a
finite sequence of the eight multiplication moves above, each strictly
decreasing

\[
 \lambda(u,v)=|u|+|v|,
\]

ends at a signed-standard pair.

### Proof

If such a sequence exists, every step is a Nielsen transformation and the
endpoint is a basis, so the input is a basis.

For the converse, use the classical Nielsen reduction theorem for a finite
generating tuple in a free group. A tuple that is not Nielsen reduced admits
an elementary product replacement that strictly shortens one component;
for a two-tuple the possible product replacements are exactly the eight
displayed moves. Iteration terminates because \(\lambda\) is a nonnegative
integer.

It remains to identify a reduced endpoint. Nielsen's endpoint theorem says a
Nielsen-reduced tuple generating a rank-\(r\) free group contains \(r\)
distinct basis letters after deletion of redundant components. Here there
are exactly two nonempty components and the generated group has rank two, so
no component is redundant and the endpoint is two distinct signed
generators. Thus it is signed standard.

Conversely, a nonbasis cannot acquire a signed-standard endpoint: every move
preserves the subgroup generated by the tuple and is invertible. \(\square\)

The theorem is an existence statement. The implementation chooses
deterministically among all strictly shorter children. This is sound because
every shorter child of a basis is again a basis; if it is nonterminal,
Theorem 2.1 supplies another shorter child. Hence an arbitrary deterministic
strict descent cannot enter a dead end when it starts at a basis.

## 3. Completeness of the bounded basis census

Fix \(C\ge2\). Enumerate every nonempty freely reduced pair \((u,v)\) with

\[
 |u|+|v|\le C.
\]

Apply Theorem 2.1 and retain exactly the pairs with a reduction witness.

### Corollary 3.1 — PROVEN

The retained pairs are exactly the ordered image pairs

\[
 (\phi(x),\phi(y))
\]

of all \(\phi\in\operatorname{Aut}(F_2)\) satisfying
\(|\phi(x)|+|\phi(y)|\le C\).

### Proof

The direct word enumeration contains every pair under the bound, independently
of any reduction path. Theorem 2.1 accepts exactly the basis pairs, and an
ordered pair is the image of \((x,y)\) under an automorphism exactly when it
is a basis. Intermediate witness lengths are irrelevant because candidates,
not paths, are bounded. \(\square\)

For \(C=4\), the certificate will prove the implementation count 200 by
regenerating and replaying every witness. The number 200 is not used to decide
whether a candidate is a basis.

## 4. Signed-permutation quotient

Let \(\Sigma\le\operatorname{Aut}(F_2)\) be the order-eight group generated by
swapping \(x,y\) and independently inverting them. For a relator pair
\(Q=(R,S)\), let \(\operatorname{canon}(Q)\) quotient:

- relator order;
- cyclic rotation of each relator;
- inversion of each relator.

Define

\[
 K(Q)=\min_{\sigma\in\Sigma}
 \operatorname{canon}(\sigma(R),\sigma(S)),
\]

using the implementation's fixed lexical order.

### Theorem 4.1 (corridor equivariance) — PROVEN

If \(K(Q)=K(Q')\), then the hidden-corridor censuses of \(Q\) and \(Q'\) with
the same word and template length bounds have:

1. the same number of accepted corridors;
2. a bijection between their accepted corridors;
3. the same multiset of output Aut(\(F_2\))-orbits;
4. the same minimum output Aut-floor.

### Proof

First consider \(\sigma\in\Sigma\). Extend \(\sigma\) to
\(F(x,y,z)\) by fixing \(z\). Map a corridor datum letterwise:

\[
 w\mapsto\sigma(w),\qquad
 I\mapsto\sigma(I).
\]

If the unique isolated occurrence in \(I\) is \(g^\varepsilon\), its image is
one occurrence of \(\sigma(g^\varepsilon)\), hence still one signed occurrence
of one basis generator. The number of \(z^{\pm1}\)-letters and both length
bounds are unchanged.

Literal substitution commutes with \(\sigma\):

\[
 \sigma\!\left(\operatorname{sub}_z(I,w)\right)
 =
 \operatorname{sub}_z(\sigma(I),\sigma(w)).
\]

Free reduction also commutes with a signed permutation. Rotating the isolator,
solving its unique basis letter, and substituting the resulting expression in
the other and defining relators are all word operations commuting with
\(\sigma\). The final convention that renames \(z\) can differ by a signed
permutation, but that is an ambient automorphism and therefore leaves the
output Aut-orbit and its floor unchanged. Applying \(\sigma^{-1}\) gives the
inverse map, so this is a bijection.

Swapping the two source relators merely swaps the two choices of source
index. Cyclic rotation or inversion of the selected source preserves the
source-orientation set. Applying either operation to the unselected relator
commutes with the eliminator substitution and changes the corresponding
output only by the same relator symmetry. Each operation is invertible.

Finally, \(K(Q)=K(Q')\) means that \(Q,Q'\) are connected by a composition of
exactly these operations. Compose their corridor bijections. \(\square\)

### Corollary 4.2 — PROVEN

It is complete to run the bounded corridor decision once per distinct
\(K(\phi(\mathrm{AK}(3)))\) among the bounded basis pairs.

For total basis-image length at most four, the certificate expects nine keys
with class sizes

```text

```

summing to 200. These are replay targets, not premises of Theorem 4.1.
